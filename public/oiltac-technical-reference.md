## Test Evidence Summary

OILTAC's performance has been evaluated across 12+ independent test campaigns conducted between 1960 and 2005 by government agencies, universities, railway operators, and power utilities in Japan, Bulgaria, Italy, Canada, and South Korea. The following are the primary citable results.

### Fuel Consumption

Independent tests across multiple engine types and applications have consistently documented fuel consumption reductions, though the magnitude varies with engine type, load conditions, and fuel grade.

**Diesel road vehicle (Italy, 1982):** At constant 60 km/h cruise, fuel consumption was reduced by approximately 6–7% following a 155 km conditioning period. Pickup performance (30–80 km/h) improved by approximately 3–4%. Test conducted by the Fuel Experimental Station, San Donato Milanese.

**Diesel engine bench test — Bulgaria (1982):** A diesel engine (Д-3900) showed 1.5–2% fuel consumption reduction under medium loads. Test conducted by V.I. Lenin Memorial Mechanical and Electrical Engineering Higher College, Sofia.

**Marine diesel long-term test (Japan, 1985–1986):** A 200-hour comparative test on an AVL-502 single-cylinder diesel engine running on A/C heavy oil blend (50:50 bunker A and C) documented approximately 2% fuel consumption reduction with OILTAC versus the untreated control. Conducted by the Marine Technical Institute, Ministry of Transportation, Japan.

**Locomotive trials:**
- Bulgarian National Railways (1982): Four locomotives with OILTAC showed 0.5–4.4% lower fuel consumption versus paired same-series controls without additive. Spectroscopic engine-oil analysis showed no adverse impact on oil quality.
- National Railroad of South Korea (1984): Two diesel trains in Seoul–Pusan mainline revenue service (multiple rolling-stock cars tracked individually) showed per-consist fuel savings of 0.6–6.1% with OILTAC, most values clustering between 1–6%. Idle fuel savings separately measured at 1.5–2%.

**Power generation — Okushiri Island (Japan, 2001–2005):** Hokkaido Electric Power Company's Okushiri Island Power Station ran OILTAC across 200 kW, 500 kW, 750 kW, and 1,000 kW diesel generator sets. Specific fuel consumption improved consistently, with Engine 5 showing approximately 5% reduction (0.279 → 0.265 L/kW) sustained over multiple fiscal years.

**Japan Railways field trial (multi-year):** Manufacturer literature confirms a detailed multi-year field trial with JR diesel rail cars resulted in documented fuel savings of "several percent," described as the most practically representative test of the product's real-world fuel economy.

### Exhaust Emissions

**Carbon monoxide (CO):** Italy-based vehicle testing using the European drive cycle showed total CO reduction of approximately 15% after a 300 km conditioning period.

**Smoke and particulate matter:** San Donato Italian testing showed smoke opacity reduced 8% at constant speed and 18.5% during acceleration from slow running. Bulgarian diesel engine testing showed 10–15% soot reduction on the Bosch scale. The Marine Technical Institute 200-hour test confirmed reduced exhaust particulate mass with OILTAC on heavy oil blend.

**NOx (Nitrogen Oxides):** Manufacturer brochure data from a bench engine test on A/C blend oil shows NOx reduced approximately 25% at higher engine loads (from ~550–600 ppm to ~400–450 ppm at approximately 6 bar BMEP). Japan Coast Guard Academy testing documented simultaneous reduction of both CO and NOx — a technically notable finding because these emissions normally trade off against each other.

**SO₂ (Sulfur Dioxide):** Bench engine data from the manufacturer brochure shows SO₂ in exhaust reduced approximately 40% at higher loads (from ~0.25% to ~0.15% at approximately 6 bar BMEP), attributed to improved combustion efficiency rather than a change in fuel sulfur content.

**Smoke incidents eliminated — power generation:** Okushiri Power Station recorded recurring soot and smoke dispersion incidents at 4–12 events per fiscal year before OILTAC use. After OILTAC was introduced, zero such incidents were recorded. When OILTAC was removed from the program, incidents returned. When reinstated, incidents ceased again. This removal/reintroduction sequence was documented across multiple fiscal years, providing a strong causal confirmation.

### Engine Deposits

The Marine Technical Institute 200-hour test measured deposit accumulation at five combustion chamber locations (cylinder head, piston top, piston side, suction valve, exhaust valve). Without OILTAC, total deposit mass was 2.4896 grams — hard, adhesive, and difficult to remove. With OILTAC on the same A/C blend fuel, total deposit mass was 3.5063 grams — but qualitatively described as very soft, dry, and easily scraped off. The deposits with OILTAC did not adhere to surfaces and did not require mechanical removal effort.

Injection nozzle fouling, boiler diffuser carbon deposition, and exhaust valve fouling were all substantially reduced in long-term trials. After one year of continuous use in an A-type fuel oil boiler, diffuser carbon deposition was described as "hardly observed."

### Engine Wear (Important Caveat)

The Marine Technical Institute 200-hour test conducted on A/C heavy oil blend documented increased piston ring wear with OILTAC (top ring: 266.3 mg without vs. 435.6 mg with; oil ring: 193.6 vs. 445.6 mg). The test authors flagged this finding, noted the cause was unclear, and recommended further testing to determine suitability and optimal conditions for heavy oil blends. Crank pin bearing wear, however, was lower with OILTAC (38.8 mg total without vs. 30.8 mg with — a 21% reduction).

This wear finding should not be generalized to standard distillate diesel operations. The A/C heavy oil blend used is a harsh fuel not representative of typical marine diesel or industrial diesel. No piston ring wear increase has been reported in standard diesel or light oil applications across the broader test corpus.

### Lubrication Oil

The Marine Technical Institute 200-hour test documented better preservation of lubricating oil total base number (TBN) with OILTAC, indicating slower oil degradation. Okushiri Power Station's long-term operation records confirmed reduced oil consumption, viscosity increase prevention, oxidation suppression, and extended oil change interval capability. Oil replenishment quantity was reduced during OILTAC use.

### Cetane Number

Japan's National Research Institute for Pollution and Resources (MITI, 1985) measured cetane number effects at three dose levels. Base gas oil cetane number was 56.0. At the standard 0.01% dose, cetane number increased to 56.5. At 0.02%, it reached 57.3. At 0.1% (10x the recommended dose), it measured 57.1. This indicates most of the available cetane improvement is captured at or near the standard dosing rate.

## Applications

OILTAC is effective across liquid petroleum fuels including distillate diesel (light oil), heavy fuel oil, A-type fuel oil, and bunker fuels (HFO/bunker C and A/C blends).

**Marine vessels:** Tugboats, port vessels, inland waterway fleets, and ocean-going vessels running on diesel or heavy fuel oil. Addresses fuel consumption, turbocharger fouling, exhaust economizer maintenance, bunker tank sludge, and injection system cleanliness.

**Rail and locomotive:** Diesel locomotive and diesel-electric rail operations. Validated in Bulgarian and Korean national railway trials.

**Power generation:** Diesel generator sets in island power, backup generation, and continuous power applications. Validated at Okushiri Island Power Station across multiple generator sizes and over five consecutive fiscal years.

**Industrial boilers and furnaces:** Burner efficiency, heating surface fouling, furnace deposit reduction, and flame quality improvement in A-type and bunker fuel boiler systems.

**Mining and heavy equipment:** Diesel-powered mobile equipment in mining operations.
